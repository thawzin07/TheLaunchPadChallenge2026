# The LaunchPad Challenge - Overview

## AI Quick Summary

The LaunchPad Challenge is a four-week, open-format AI build sprint for students in Singapore. There is no fixed theme. Teams choose a serious AI project or research idea, build it during the challenge period, submit a repository, short demo video, and 1,000-word write-up, then get judged by domain-relevant professionals. The challenge is designed less like a normal hackathon and more like a career-signal system: judges evaluate whether the team is sharp and thoughtful enough to interview.

## What To Build

Anything serious that uses AI. Strong categories include:

- Applications: products or tools solving real problems for real users.
- Agentic systems: multi-step, tool-using, autonomous or semi-autonomous workflows.
- Embodied AI and robotics: perception, control, sim-to-real, hardware-in-the-loop, or hardware-related AI work.
- Deep learning research: new methods, architectures, training strategies, or rigorous empirical studies.
- Infrastructure and tooling: evals, observability, data pipelines, developer tools, or systems that improve AI workflows.

Projects are judged within their own domain. A research project is not expected to have a polished UI, and an application is not expected to introduce new theory.

## Judging Concept

The challenge is built around evidence and reasoning, not polish.

- More than 100 professionals are expected to judge submissions.
- Judges include founders, engineers, researchers, product leaders, and domain specialists.
- Submissions are matched to judges based on expertise.
- Judges review the write-up, demo video, and repository spot-checks.
- Judges score five pillars from 1 to 5.
- Each judge also answers: "Would I want to interview this person?"

The five judging pillars are:

1. Problem
2. Approach
3. Evidence
4. Constraints
5. Honesty and Trajectory

Main rule: evidence must match the claim. A modest claim that is proven is better than a large claim that is only asserted.

## Rules And Integrity

- Original work: projects should be substantially built during the challenge period.
- Prior work is allowed only if the write-up clearly states what existed before and what is new.
- Judges score the delta for extensions of prior work.
- Attribute all third-party code, models, datasets, and assets.
- AI coding assistants and sponsor APIs are allowed and encouraged.
- Participants remain accountable for every claim they make.
- Fabricated results, doctored demos, or misleading evaluations can lead to disqualification.
- One submission per team.
- The version submitted by the deadline is what gets judged.
- Late submissions are not accepted.
- Repository, video, and write-up links must remain accessible throughout judging.
- Do not contact judges about the submission during judging.
- Submissions must not contain harmful content or violate laws or platform terms.

## Practical Strategy

For a strong submission, build the project around the rubric from day one:

- Define success criteria before building.
- Keep a decision log for major technical choices.
- Run a simple baseline early.
- Measure cost, latency, quality, reliability, or other relevant constraints as you build.
- Save failed attempts and negative results.
- Write claims only when evidence exists.
- Keep the final write-up concise and evidence-backed.
